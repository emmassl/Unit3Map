Looking for
Doug B

A trail of 250 stickers, five-dollar candles,
and a parking lot in Branford, CT.

By Emma Slagle

APRIL 21, 2026

I'm someone that takes a lot of photos, and I mean a lot.

Most are of things others might just walk past, brushing off as peculiar— like a sculpture of a crouching body with a giant tongue for a torso, planted on a pier sidewalk in East Boston.

Or a wall decal inside a stall of the women's bathroom in the Yale School of Art, narrating the potential experiences one might have while relieving oneself.

Or this cry for help disguised in the form of a flyer calling for small teacups...

I take note of these things, I document them, and I have since first getting a phone in the 6th grade (hence the 200,000+ image camera roll).

It was only natural then, that I finally snapped a photo of my first “Doug B Candles” sticker this past fall, walking down a street in East Rock— in the same typewriter font I'd been seeing in passing since freshman year.

Holding no website, phone number, or QR code, just a name, a product, and an immense air of intrigue— who is Doug B, and how is he selling these candles?

After taking this photo, my curiosity grew. I kept noticing these stickers, more intensely than before, and more than I could ignore. I needed to find Doug B, and the first step lay in navigating and narrowing down his territory. How many stickers were there, and could they lead me to Doug himself? I enlisted my friend Cade to traverse the city with me to find out.

We started at the corner of Prospect and Sachem, and found our first Doug B sticker within minutes on a parking pole.

As we continued to walk north, the numbers only bloomed.

Some stickers even appeared in tight groups right next to one another, almost impossible to miss.

Most of the stickers were legible, but on State street, they were different, peeled at the corners, faded, some torn down the middle. Many were almost gone, just residue left behind. We didn't know if someone's been removing them, or if they're just older than the rest. Either way, State street felt like the edge of something.

Making our way further south, the numbers continued to multiply: on lampposts, telephone poles, electrical boxes, on the sides of crosswalk buttons, when we thought it couldn't get any more populated, we encountered the Court Street bridge. Every railing. One after another, all the way across, on both sides, filled with Doug B stickers.

By the end of the walk, we had counted two hundred and fifty. East Rock, Little Italy, Wooster. Just about three and a half hours, 250 stickers along a single afternoon's path. We found Doug B territory, but still no Doug B.

I asked more than 30 friends, colleagues and New Haven locals, and only two had noticed this sticker before, nobody knew who Doug B Candles was. In 2026, how do you sell something with just a name and a product?

I turned to the internet to find out more, and it turns out there, I wasn’t the only one curious about Doug B. Scattered across Reddit and Facebook, strangers had been quietly marveling at him for years.

Even on the forums, Doug had already become something mythical. One user compared him to the Easter Bunny.

And was building a reputation.

Buried one reddit thread was my first clue, rumoring he frequents Willoughbby's, a coffee shop in Branford.

So I called Willoughby's. They knew who he was, but he didn't sell there, and they weren't even sure if he was still active. They suggested I try calling Common Grounds.

Common Grounds had claimed they never heard of him— it was a short conversation. They hung up, and I was back at square one.

Another Facebook post mentioned that his candles were sold at Nuzzo's Farm, a wedding venue. I called, and they knew him, but emphasized they had no affiliation. Another dead end, and by this point, I could feel there was something nobody was telling me, a story or moment about our guy I just wasn't privy to yet.

The last rumor was the most specific: someone on Facebook had posted that Doug hangs around Branford Stop & Shop around 9 p.m. I called the store. They transferred me to the manager. She told me he was well known around there, but she just couldn't explain why. "You could try calling the Branford police for more information," she said.

I did not call the Branford police.

At last I found my most essential clue on a Facebook forum, Doug's number.

So I called. No answer. I called again. Still nothing. Then, less than a minute later, my phone rang.

Hello, who is this?
Hi, this is Emma— is this Doug B?
Yes.
Great! I've been seeing your stickers around New Haven, and I was hoping to buy a candle.
Oh yeah, I just got concerned because my phone said you were in Hawaii.
Yes, sorry, I am from Hawaii.
I thought you were spam.
No, I live in New Haven now.
Okay. Well, what candle are you looking for?
I read you have a really lovely nice white rose.
Oh, I'll have to check— the scents come from China and I might be out. Where exactly in New Haven are you?
East Rock.
Oh, well I'm in Branford.
I'm fine with meeting in New Haven, or I can come out.
I usually meet people— you know where Nonna's restaurant is in Branford?
No, but I can find it.
It's right across from the town dump. I usually meet people in the parking lot there. Do you like Christmas tree?
Yes, but I'm more into floral scents.
Floral, okay. I just made a large batch, and they sell out quickly. I'll make you a floral one. I'll call you when it's done— should be a day or two. Thank you for calling, Emma.

He called back the next day. We confirmed a cash-only 5 p.m. pickup at Nonna Gina's, the day after that.

I brought Cade, along with our friend Kristjan to drive us out. Five p.m, Thursday, Nonna Gina's parking lot, right across from the Branford town dump, right on time, exactly where he said it would be, and Doug was already there when we pulled in. He was wearing Doug B head to toe— a "Doug B Candles" hat, t-shirt, both with that same typewriter font I'd been following around New Haven for months, and even his jeans were marked "Doug B" in sharpie. We learned Doug B was an an ex-trucker turned candle man, and ended up standing in the parking lot for almost thirty minutes takling with him about 80s music, The Cure, The Smiths, Culture Club, and his immense passion for it. I asked him how the stickers got everywhere, and how he'd managed it, but he wouldn't say. "I don't know, little minions. I don't disclose the secret." he told me. "It gets people interested.

My white rose & peach candle

It smells like sweet, sappy, heaven. What does it mean when a guy can build a brand this way — through analogue marketing, stickers, almost no information attached? Welcome to the conclusion: the territory of Doug. Doug B sells five-dollar candles out of a parking lot in Branford. His stickers are everywhere in New Haven, at least 250 of them along a single afternoon's walk, but almost nobody knows his name. In an era when every small business lives online, Doug has built a local legend the old way: one adhesive at a time, hidden in plain sight, spread mostly word of mouth. More so than the stickers, I think its Doug himself that draws people to his candles.
